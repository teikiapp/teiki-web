Who makes Teiki

Teiki is built by one person. Here's why it exists and how it's run.

Why I built it

I'm Peter. I've worked as a software engineer for 12 years, and I build Teiki on my own under the name CapyDev.

I started it because none of the trackers I tried felt good to use. The one I stuck with longest worked fine. There was nothing really wrong with it, but the interface felt dated, and I wanted something better to look at.

What I was after was simple: my subscriptions in one place, at a glance, with a calendar I'd actually enjoy opening. I also liked the idea of colored tiles carrying each service's own app icon, so you find Netflix or Spotify by its logo, the way you would on your home screen, instead of reading down a column of text. That became the shape of the app.

How it's run

Teiki is a side project, not a company. Updates arrive when they're ready rather than on a schedule, and the app stays small because small is what I wanted from it in the first place.

It should also be useful the moment you open it, with no account to create first. What Teiki stores, and the few outside services it relies on, are written out plainly in the privacy policy.

Missing a service?

Teiki ships with a catalog of subscription services, and it will never be complete. There are always local providers, smaller apps, and services I've never heard of.

If the one you pay for isn't in there, don't be shy about telling me. I'm happy to add it, and it's a quick change on my end. Send it through the Submit a Service page.
